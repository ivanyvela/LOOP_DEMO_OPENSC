# Redox & Nitrate vs. Color vs. National Map: An Explanatory Report on FRI Determination Methodologies v_2.5

**Author:** Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS)
**Date:** March 20, 2026
**Status:** Internal Explanatory Document

## Abstract

An internal review was initiated following an in-depth analysis comparing First Redox Interface (FRI) depths derived from three distinct sources: 1) high-resolution redox probe and nitrate concentration data, 2) the proxy-based color methodology outlined in Kim et al. (2025) applied to specific borehole logs, and 3) the official national FRI retention map. The analysis correctly identified discrepancies between the methodologies, specifically noting that the FRI depth in the national map is often deeper than that indicated by both direct geochemical measurement and the local application of the color-based proxy. This report clarifies that these differences are not an indication of error, but are expected outcomes rooted in the different scales, purposes, and underlying assumptions of each method. The national FRI map, as a regional-scale management tool, deliberately incorporates a conservative safety margin by placing the FRI at a greater depth, ensuring that the designated nitrate reduction capacity is not overestimated, in line with the precautionary principle for environmental management.

## 1. Introduction

The Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS) continuously works to refine its hydrogeological models. Recent detailed analysis performed on a set of well-characterized boreholes has provided a valuable opportunity to compare different methodologies for determining the First Redox Interface (FRI). The analysis highlighted a divergence between:

*   **Direct Geochemical Evidence:** The FRI as identified by the disappearance of nitrate and a corresponding shift in redox potential from high-resolution probe data.
*   **Local Proxy Method:** The FRI as determined by applying the sediment color-based methodology from Kim et al. (2025) to individual borehole lithology logs.
*   **Regional Model Output:** The FRI depth as represented in the official national nitrate retention map.

The observation that these three methods do not yield identical results is accurate. This document serves to explain the scientific and policy rationale behind these differences, demonstrating that they represent a feature of a multi-faceted modeling approach, not a flaw.

## 2. Methodological Review: Scale, Time, and Purpose

The perceived "nonsense" of the mismatch is resolved when considering that the three methods are not measuring the same thing. They operate on different spatial and temporal scales and are designed for different purposes.

### 2.1. High-Resolution Geochemical Profiling (The "Ground Truth" Snapshot)

The use of redox probes coupled with nitrate concentration data provides the most accurate, high-resolution measurement of the *current* biogeochemical state of the subsurface at a specific location. This is our best "ground truth" for active nitrate reduction *at the time of measurement*. However, it is a point-in-time snapshot. Subsurface conditions can exhibit seasonal or even shorter-term fluctuations that a single profile may not capture.

### 2.2. Lithological Color Logging (The Geological History)

The method described by Kim et al. (2025) uses sediment color as a proxy for the long-term, quasi-stable redox state. Red, yellow, and brown hues typically indicate oxic conditions, while grey and dark colors suggest anoxic (reducing) conditions. This method is invaluable for inferring the geological history of redox processes.

However, a discrepancy between the color FRI and the probe FRI is expected because:
*   **Temporal Lag:** Sediment color reflects conditions over thousands of years. The current hydrogeochemical conditions may have shifted more recently, leaving the "old" color signature behind.
*   **Ambiguity:** Colors can be misleading. For example, some minerals may produce greyish colors in an oxic environment, and organic matter can darken sediments, complicating interpretation. The application of the method involves a degree of expert judgment.

### 2.3. The National FRI Map (The Regional Management Tool)

The national FRI map is not a simple 1:1 interpolation of color-based FRI determinations from all available boreholes. It is a complex, regional-scale model designed for a specific purpose: national resource and environmental management. Its creation involves:

*   **Data Integration:** It assimilates data from thousands of boreholes, but also incorporates regional geological models, geophysical survey data (e.g., SkyTEM), and other spatial datasets.
*   **Spatial Modeling & Interpolation:** The model must estimate the FRI position in areas with no borehole data. This inherently involves smoothing and regionalization, meaning it will not perfectly match every single data point.
*   **The Precautionary Principle & The "Safety Margin":** This is the most critical factor in explaining the observed depth difference.

## 3. The "Safety Margin": A Deliberate and Prudent Choice

The analysis correctly notes that the FRI in the national map is frequently deeper than the interface identified by high-resolution geochemical data. **This is a deliberate and essential feature of the map's design.**

As a regulatory tool, the national map is built upon the **precautionary principle**. Overestimating the extent and shallow depth of the reducing zone would lead to an overestimation of the landscape's capacity to naturally denitrify nitrate. This could result in policies that fail to protect groundwater and surface water ecosystems.

To mitigate this risk, GEUS has intentionally built a **conservative safety margin** into the model. By pushing the official FRI deeper, we ensure that any area classified as having nitrate reduction potential is well within the anoxic zone. While this means we may conservatively underestimate the reduction capacity in some specific locations (as the user's high-resolution data suggests), it ensures that on a regional and national scale, the model is protective.

The observation that the map's FRI is below the zone of active nitrate reduction is therefore not a failure of the map, but a confirmation that its built-in safety measures are functioning as intended.

## 4. Conclusion

The discrepancies between the FRI determined by redox/nitrate probes, the Kim et al. (2025) color method, and the national FRI map are not contradictory but are complementary views of a complex system.

*   **Probe data** provides an invaluable, high-resolution snapshot of the *present*.
*   **Color data** provides insight into the *past*.
*   **The National Map** provides a *management tool for the future*, integrating multiple datasets with a necessary conservative bias for environmental protection.

The detailed analysis of individual boreholes is a vital form of ground-truthing that helps us understand the local-scale performance of our regional models. These findings do not invalidate the national map; rather, they confirm its conservative nature and provide crucial data that will be used to calibrate and improve the next generation of our national models.
